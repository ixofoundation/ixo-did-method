## Status of This Document

This document is a **draft specification** for the `did:ixo` Decentralized Identifier method, submitted for inclusion in the W3C Decentralized Identifier Extensions registry. It defines how the IXO blockchain implements the W3C Decentralized Identifiers (DIDs) v1.0 specification [[DID-CORE]](#ref-did-core) using the Interchain Identifier (IID) framework.

Feedback and contributions are welcomed via the issue tracker at the repository above.

## Abstract

Interchain Identifiers (IIDs) are a family of Decentralized Identifier methods purpose-designed to identify, refer to, and interact with digital Domains within blockchain namespaces. The **IXO DID Method** (`did:ixo`) is an implementation of the IID specification, anchored on the IXO blockchain.

The `did:ixo` method builds on the W3C Decentralized Identifier (DID) Core specification [[DID-CORE]](#ref-did-core). IXO DIDs are fully conformant DIDs and IXO DID Documents are conformant DID Documents. Unlike generic DIDs, IXO DIDs only reference on-chain entities, referred to as **Domains**. These Domains identify and provide verifiable records for any type of digital entity, including NFTs, fungible tokens, tokenized namespace records, and other Spatial Web entities. This includes "digital twin" representations of real-world physical or virtual entities, as well as representations of abstract or intangible *Cognitive Digital Twin* systems, such as projects.

By restricting IXO DIDs to on-chain assets, this method creates a class of identifier uniquely suited to the requirements of decentralized digital Domains that are created, controlled, and interfaced with through Web 3.0 and Web 2.0 services.

The `did:ixo` method also introduces conformant extensions to the DID Core specification that provide privacy-respecting options for a comprehensive range of Domain properties and functionalities, including Linked Resources, Service Endpoints, Accorded Rights, Linked Claims, Linked Entities, and Blockchain Accounts.

DID Methods that conform to the IID specification resolve to DID Documents representing how to securely interact with a uniquely identified digital Domain within a unique blockchain namespace. The fact that IXO DIDs are DIDs means that software applications conformant with the W3C specification can interoperate with IXO DIDs and IXO DID Documents, although some IID-specific features may require additional tooling.

## Introduction

Decentralized Identifiers (DIDs) are a W3C standard for secure identifiers without dependency on a trusted third party. DIDs are used to reference any subject, including people, organizations, physical locations, and physical and digital assets.

IXO DIDs define a family of DID methods used to locate on-chain Domains and their associated resources, services, claims, rights, graphs of linked entities, and accounts. These Domains can be addressed and used across namespaces: from the web, from another chain, or from any DID-compliant wallet or application.

The IXO DID method allows for customizing DIDs to meet the need for secure interactions with on-chain Domains anchored to the IXO blockchain.

### Built on URIs

IXO DIDs build on a foundation of Internet identifiers, dating back to Uniform Resource Locators and Uniform Resource Identifiers [[RFC3986]](#ref-rfc3986), which are the foundation of the World Wide Web.

URIs allow for different mechanisms to be used for interpreting and applying the syntax of the identifier. For example, `http://example.com` specifies a web-based resource retrieved using HTTP, whereas `mailto:joe@example.com` specifies a resource that can receive email messages using SMTP.

Decentralized Identifiers (DIDs) build on URIs and URLs to provide a new class of URIs that afford secure interaction with the controller of that identifier, without reliance on a trusted third party.

### DID and IID Documents

A DID such as:

```
did:ixo:abc123
```

resolves to a DID Document describing how to securely interact with the controller and properties of the DID.

All DIDs *resolve* to their associated **DID Document**, which contains verification methods and relationships, and the service endpoints that secure interaction with the controller of the asset.

**Verification Methods**, such as public keys, may be used to establish verification relationships for the purpose of authentication, assertion, capability delegation, or capability invocation.

DID-URLs extend the base DID syntax to support `/path`, `?query`, and `#fragment` parts. IXO DIDs use the `/path` and `#fragment` parts for IID References and IID Resources.

### Domains as Tokenized Digital Assets

The IID specification defines how Domains may be tokenized as digital assets that can be:

1. Identified as unique digital assets of a specific type and class of tokens.
2. Linked to verifiable on-chain and off-chain resources.
3. Accorded rights in relation to both the token and its linked resources, including machine-executable rights that may be issued and delegated off-chain using a mechanism such as Authorization Capabilities (zCaps), for accessing, invoking, and transacting with both on-chain and off-chain services.
4. Generate verifiable claims about the asset, or make claims in relation to other Domains.
5. Link to other entities in the Spatial Web with a defined graph of nodes and their edge relationships with other entities.
6. Own and control blockchain accounts that may hold any forms of digital assets, including Group Accounts that are controlled through on-chain governance mechanisms.
7. Afforded privacy in relation to the token's metadata, including what the token represents, information about its linked resources, and the services which can be invoked.
8. Transferred with verifiable provenance across registry systems.

## Conformance

The key words MAY, MUST, MUST NOT, OPTIONAL, RECOMMENDED, REQUIRED, SHALL, SHALL NOT, SHOULD, and SHOULD NOT in this document are to be interpreted as described in [[RFC2119]](#ref-rfc2119) when, and only when, they appear in all capitals.

Implementations MUST conform to the W3C DID Core specification [[DID-CORE]](#ref-did-core). All IXO DID Documents MUST be conformant DID Documents as defined in [[DID-CORE]](#ref-did-core).

This document defines conformance requirements for:

- **IXO DID Implementers**: Software that creates, resolves, updates, and deactivates `did:ixo` DIDs.
- **IXO DID Resolvers**: Software that resolves `did:ixo` DIDs to DID Documents.
- **IXO DID Documents**: DID Documents produced by resolving `did:ixo` DIDs.

## Terminology

1. **DIDs** — Decentralized Identifiers [[DID-CORE]](#ref-did-core). Cryptographically secured global identifiers defined by the DID Core specification.

2. **DID-URLs** — An extended form of a DID with additional components such as **/path**, **?query**, and **#fragment** appended to a base DID.

3. **DID Document** — A representation of the metadata needed to securely interact with a DID. Retrieved by DID resolution, DID Documents contain verification relationships and methods, service endpoints, and other method-specific properties.

4. **Resolution** — The process by which one retrieves or generates a DID Document for a given DID. Resolution and other DID operations are method-specific and may vary depending on the design decisions of each particular method.

5. **Dereference** — The process of retrieving a resource, given a URL. Once the DID for a DID-URL is resolved, the returned DID Document provides the information needed to retrieve the resource (if any) represented by the DID-URL.

6. **IID** — Interchain Identifier. A DID that conforms to both the DID Core specification and the IID specification defined in this document.

7. **Domain** — An on-chain entity identified by an IXO DID. Domains may represent NFTs, fungible tokens, smart contracts, governance entities, projects, or any other addressable on-chain asset on the IXO blockchain.

8. **IID Document** — A DID Document that conforms to both the DID Core specification and the IID specification, including IID-specific properties.

9. **Linked Resource** — A verifiable, potentially privacy-preserving reference to a digital resource associated with a Domain, specified in the `linkedResource` property of an IID Document.

10. **Accorded Right** — A right conferred upon the controller of an IXO DID, specified in the `accordedRight` property of an IID Document.

## 1. Method Name

The name string that identifies this DID method is: **`ixo`**

A DID that uses this method MUST begin with the prefix `did:ixo:`. This prefix is case-sensitive per [[DID-CORE]](#ref-did-core).

## 2. Method-Specific DID Syntax

The IXO DID method defines the following ABNF grammar for `did:ixo` identifiers, extending the DID Core grammar [[DID-CORE]](#ref-did-core):

```abnf
ixo-did            = "did:ixo:" ixo-specific-id
ixo-specific-id    = *( ixo-id-component ":" ) ixo-id-component
ixo-id-component   = 1*idchar
idchar             = ALPHA / DIGIT / "." / "-" / "_"
```

The `ixo-specific-id` is composed of one or more components separated by colons. By convention, IXO uses lowercase identifiers. Valid examples of `did:ixo` DIDs include:

```
did:ixo:abc123
did:ixo:entity:abc123
did:ixo:entity:abc123xyz
```

IXO DID-URLs extend the base IXO DID syntax to include path, query, and fragment components, following [[RFC3986]](#ref-rfc3986):

```abnf
ixo-did-url    = ixo-did path-abempty [ "?" query ] [ "#" fragment ]

path-abempty   = *( "/" segment )

query          = *( pchar / "/" / "?" )

fragment       = *( pchar / "/" / "?" )

pchar          = unreserved / pct-encoded / sub-delims / ":" / "@"
```

IXO DID-URLs are used to construct:

- **IID References**: DID-URLs with a fragment part (e.g., `did:ixo:abc123#key-1`), used to refer to resources defined within an IXO DID Document.
- **IID Resources**: DID-URLs with a path part (e.g., `did:ixo:abc123/resource.png`), used to retrieve external resources linked to the Domain.

## 3. IID Extensions

The `did:ixo` method extends the DID Core specification with new properties, property values, and terminology. These extensions are defined in the IXO JSON-LD context:

```
https://w3id.org/ixo/ns/interchain-identifiers/v1
```

### 3.1 New Terminology

1. **IID References** are DID-URLs with a fragment part, e.g., `did:ixo:abc#123`. These refer to resources associated with the IID subject, defined in the IID Document with an `id` property matching the fragment term. This reference SHOULD be used in RDF statements about IID References; the raw IID SHOULD be used in RDF statements about the IID asset itself.

2. **IID Linked Resources** are DID-URLs with a path part, e.g., `did:ixo:abc/123`. These are used to retrieve online resources linked to the on-chain asset. They are defined in the `linkedResource` property of the IID Document using both a `path` property and an `id` property.

3. **IID Domains** — All IXO DIDs refer to on-chain Domains. The DID Subject of an IXO DID is the IID Domain. These terms may be used interchangeably.

#### HTTPRange14

By convention, all downloadable linked resources may be referred to by their associated IID Reference `did:ixo:abc#123` and downloaded using their IID Resource URL `did:ixo:abc/123`.

This is achieved by using the same lexical term for the path part in the `path` property and the fragment part in the `id` property.

By taking this approach, IXO DIDs avoid the HTTPRange14 problem [[HTTPRANGE14]](#ref-httprange14) by not conflating the same string as a conceptual identifier (as a URI) in RDF statements, with that same string used to retrieve a specific online resource (as a URL).

### 3.2 New Properties and Values

1. **`linkedResource`** is a new IID Document property for specifying how to verify, and optionally retrieve, any and all resources necessary for the proper functioning of the on-chain asset. Like email attachments, Linked Resources provide for attaching arbitrary media to an on-chain asset.

2. **`transclude`** is a new IID Document property for specifying where in an IID Document to transclude a linked resource. If present, the value of this property MUST be one (or an array of more than one) Linked Resource that eventually dereferences to a raw JSON-LD object. The properties of that JSON-LD object will be injected into the current IID Document, replacing the `transclude` property entirely. The properties of the transcluded JSON-LD MUST be transformed to their absolute representation using the object's `@context` value prior to transclusion. The associated linked resources MUST have a `rel` value of `"extension"` and a `mediaType` value of `"application/ld+json"`.

3. **`extension`** (a type of Linked Resource): A JSON-LD extension of the current document. The RDF statements in the extension are to be included in the current IID Document, where specified by a `transclude` property. For example, additional service endpoint definitions may be added in a linked resource. These endpoints can be verified as being associated with the IID, but only by those parties who secure the definitions through other privacy-respecting mechanisms. This property standardizes how to verifiably move arbitrary RDF statements outside of the IID Document context, to provide additional security and privacy.

4. **`executableRight`** (a type of Linked Resource): This resource is a machine-executable capability that can be invoked by the IID owner or its delegate, using cryptographic materials defined elsewhere in the IID Document `verificationMethod` property.

5. **`assertion`** (a `rel` value for a Linked Resource): Verifiable credentials, verified claims, and claim tokens as described in NFT-RFC-008 [[NFT-RFC-008]](#ref-nft-rfc-008). This allows arbitrary, yet verifiable attestations to be made either about the asset or about the resources defined by IID References. The attributes represented in these claims can be retrieved through the token interface using a Query by Example (graph query) mechanism.

6. **`rel`** (a property of Linked Resource): Defines the relationship of this resource to the IID asset. Known values include:
   - `"evidence"` — The resource is evidence for the creation of the asset.
   - `"encodedRepresentation"` — The resource is a binary representation of the asset, interpretable by compatible software to display or interact with.
   - `"visualRepresentation"` — The resource is a visual representation of the asset.

7. **`accordedRight`** (a type of relationship to a Linked Resource): Specifies the rights accorded to the IID owner, or its delegate, which may be executed by physical-world institutions or processes. Such as a digital driver's license according certain rights to drive, or a theater ticket according access to a show. The representation framework for such rights must be non-prescriptive, including both plain text statements of rights (e.g., "The controller of this IID is entitled to..."), or more rigorous and computationally evaluatable RDF statements, describing the range of benefits accompanying the basic rights of the token.

8. **`displayName`** (a property of Linked Resources): Gives a brief textual label for display.

9. **`displayDescription`** (a property of Linked Resources): A longer text phrase for displaying additional detail about the asset.

10. **`displayIcon`** (a property of Linked Resources): A URL for an image asset to use when displaying the asset.

### 3.3 Linked Resources

The `linkedResource` property provides a privacy-enabled way to attach digital resources to an on-chain Domain. This is an optional property which may contain one or more resource descriptors in an array. This property provides the metadata required for accessing and using the specified resource, such as the type of resource, a proof to verify the resource, and a service endpoint for requesting and retrieving the resource.

Resources may be provided in-line or by secure reference. Inline resources are appropriate only for use cases that need to directly include the resource in the IID Document. In many cases, this is a privacy concern. However, for some use cases, resources must be specified for on-chain execution, which justifies the added bytes and potential disclosure risk. The resource descriptor provides for a flexible representation of various MIME types, compression, and encoding, as required for the use case.

This approach allows token owners to manage privacy in three key ways:

1. Avoids posting potentially sensitive information on-chain in an unavoidably public and irrevocable manner.

2. Provides a service endpoint that can apply appropriate privacy and security checks before revealing information.

3. The hashgraph resource descriptor type obscures not only the content of the linked resource, but also the quantity of resource objects.

Resources may be secured by specifying a `proofType` of `hash` or `hashgraph`. A hashgraph uses a Merkle tree of hashes for external content associated with this asset. A resource descriptor of this type obscures both the type and the number of such resources, while allowing each such resource to be verifiably linked to the asset. It also provides for privacy-respecting verification of complete disclosure. Anyone who needs to prove they have all of the linked resources can compare their own hash graph of resources with the value stored in the IID Document. Note that this anti-censorship technique requires a verifier to discover the type and nature of those resources independently.

Proposed properties for resource descriptors in the `linkedResource` property:

```json
{
  "linkedResource": [{
    "path": "/myResource.png",
    "id": "#myResource.png",
    "rel": "visualRepresentation",
    "type": "nft:ResourceDescriptor",
    "proof": [{
      "type": "hash",
      "stage": "raw",
      "value": "<multihash-value>"
    }],
    "resourceFormat": "image/png",
    "compression": "none",
    "encryption": [],
    "encoding": "multibase",
    "endpoint": "did:ixo:abc123?service=mediator",
    "resource": "..."
  }]
}
```

The properties of a resource descriptor are:

- **`path`** (optional): IID Resource path for this resource in the asset namespace, e.g., `/myResource.png`.
- **`id`** (optional): IID Reference ID for this resource, e.g., `#myResource.png`.
- **`rel`** (optional): The relationship of this resource to the IID Asset.
- **`type`**: The JSON-LD type of this resource.
- **`proof`**: An array of proofs, any of which may be used. Each proof object has:
  - **`type`** (optional): `"hash"` | `"hashgraph"` | `"hashset"` — the form of proof used to verify the resource as authentic.
  - **`stage`** (optional): `"raw"` | `"compressed"` | `"encrypted"` | `"encoded"` — the stage at which the proof was computed.
  - **`value`** (optional): The actual proof value for this particular resource.
- **`resourceFormat`**: The IANA media type of the linked resource.
- **`compression`** (optional): `"gzip"` | `"none"` — the compression applied to the raw resource.
- **`encryption`** (optional): The encryption technique applied after compression and before encoding (open-ended for extensibility).
- **`encoding`** (optional): `"native"` | `"multibase"` | `"string"` — the encoding of an inline resource; `"native"` means the inline resource is native JSON-LD with neither compression nor encryption.
- **`endpoint`** (optional): A URL at which this resource can be retrieved before decrypting and decompressing.
- **`resource`** (optional): A representation of the resource for inline distribution, encoded per the `encoding` property, then compressed per `compression`, then encrypted per `encryption`.

### 3.4 New Service Types

#### Polymorphic Mediator

A polymorphic mediator is an endpoint that can act on behalf of any number of services, depending on how it is called, for any number of people. This allows a single endpoint to be specified for arbitrary sets of services with excellent herd privacy characteristics. The best practice for such a mediator is to act as a reverse-proxy, rather than as a redirect: the response should be sent directly through the mediator rather than redirecting the request to a different server.

As a mediator, this service provides a secret mapping between the public endpoint and the eventual actual service for any number of parties. The responses provided by the mediator are signed by the IID controller and optionally encrypted for the recipient by the IID controller, ensuring that the mediator can neither tamper with nor even see the response (when encrypted). It is anticipated that individuals will pay mediators they trust to get an extra level of privacy by mixing their endpoint transactions with others.

As a polymorphic endpoint, the mediator is configurable for handling any number of services, such as:

- **Negotiator Endpoint**: A service for negotiating mutually agreeable communications channels, preferably using private set intersection. The output of negotiation is a communication channel and whatever credentials may be needed to access it.
- **Confidential Storage**: A secure data storage endpoint using end-to-end encryption and zCaps for authorization.
- **Authorization Server**: A UMA or GNAP style authorization server endpoint.
- **Resource Server**: A simple HTTP server such as might host a blog or company site.
- **Messaging Service**: A messaging service using a particular protocol such as Signal, SMTP, XMPP, or IRC.

## 4. IXO DID Architecture

### 4.1 IIDs as DIDs

Decentralized Identifiers (DIDs) are a standards-track specification developed by the World Wide Web Consortium [[DID-CORE]](#ref-did-core). They are "a new type of globally unique identifier designed to enable individuals and organizations to generate our own identifiers using systems we trust, and to prove control of those identifiers (authenticate) using cryptographic proofs (for example, digital signatures)."

DIDs enable verifiable, decentralized digital identity. They use an RFC3986-compatible syntax, making DIDs URIs designed to work within existing web frameworks.

DID-URLs support additional **path** (`/directory/file`), **query** (`?query=value`), and **fragment** (`#fragment`) parts. Fragments are commonly used to refer to elements within a DID Document.

DIDs work because of several structural design choices.

**First**, each DID specifies a DID Method, which defines how a DID consumer might perform Create, Read, Update, and Deactivate operations. Different types of DIDs may have different Methods, and each Method may define unique approaches for those functions. This allows DIDs anchored to Bitcoin and Ethereum, as well as bespoke fit-for-purpose ledgers such as Veres One or Sovrin. DID Methods MAY be registered in a common registry for increased interoperability, but such registration is not required.

**Second**, every DID resolves to a DID Document, which contains all the metadata required for interacting securely with the DID Subject:

- Public Keys
- Verification Relationships
  - Assertion Method
  - Authentication Method
  - Capability Delegation
  - Capability Invocation
- Service endpoints

**Third**, DID Document state is managed by DID Registries. In the case of `did:ixo`, the IXO blockchain serves as the DID Registry. This is an inspectable, auditable ledger that provides verifiability and, with some effort, reversibility of changes in case of inappropriate transactions.

**Fourth**, DID Documents are defined using an abstract data model, allowing any number of serializations, with initial support for JSON and JSON-LD serializations. CBOR and CBOR-LD are also anticipated for their ability to dramatically reduce the size of DID Documents.

This specification leverages DIDs for all of those benefits by defining IXO DIDs as a profile of DIDs. IXO DIDs **are** DIDs. IXO DID Documents **are** DID Documents. The IXO DID method **is** a DID Method. The IXO namespace for IXO DIDs is defined by this specification, which specifies how to perform the create, read, update, and deactivate operations. This IID family of DID Methods shares a common purpose and leverages shared property definitions for improved cross-chain interoperability.

Because DIDs are URIs, IXO DIDs are also URIs, making IXO DIDs natively compliant with existing RFC3986-compliant libraries, including those used for RDF and the World Wide Web.

It is useful to note that Verification Methods can be anything (e.g., Ed25519, Secp256k1, etc.), and that IXO DID subjects are always on-chain assets. Authenticating on a token's behalf is taken to indicate proof-of-ownership.

In the examples below, the CRUD operations of the DID are managed by method-specific means based on Ed25519. The on-chain state — referred to as the "registry" in DID Core — can be inspected to deterministically generate the IXO DID Document. Different registries provide their own specific ways to inspect state, and different rules for deterministically generating DID Documents. This variability is handled in the IXO DID method, as described in the DID Operations section of this specification.

### 4.2 Extensibility

IXO DIDs use three mechanisms for extensibility, following the DID Extensibility approach described in Section 4.1 of the DID Core Specification.

**First**, IID methods MAY be created for any existing or new blockchain or distributed system. Each IID method MUST specify the mechanisms for a DID user to create, read, update, and deactivate IIDs of that method. There is no technological requirement to register IID Methods, so no central authority or permission should be required to define an IID method.

**Second**, JSON-LD enables unambiguous use of JSON properties through the addition or modification of `@context` properties. This approach allows any RDF statement to be losslessly represented in JSON, avoiding potential confusion between properties with the same name being used differently by different organizations. Following the DID Core specification, IID Documents which implement the JSON-LD representation already have a `@context` property `"https://www.w3.org/ns/did/v1"` as the first value. The IID specification adds a second `@context` property that links to this IID Specification.

Like any DID method, any IID Method may add properties in a decentralized way by creating their own JSON-LD context file, publishing it online, and adding it as a third `@context`. For example, the context property for the IXO customization of IIDs includes:

```json
"@context": [
  "https://www.w3.org/ns/did/v1",
  "https://w3id.org/ixo/ns/interchain-identifiers/v1",
  "https://www.w3id.org/ixo/ns/context/v1"
]
```

**Third**, a context may be added that links to another IID, allowing the Domain to inherit the properties of another Domain. For instance, IXO defines classes of Domains, such as Protocol Domains, that provide the settings for all instantiated child Domains. This inheritance may flow through a directed acyclic graph (DAG) of parent-child Domains, creating a system of nested Domains. It also enables Domains to be autonomously and independently created that share the same property sets of a parent class. This IXO `class` property follows the following pattern:

```json
"@context": [
  "https://www.w3.org/ns/did/v1",
  "https://w3id.org/ixo/ns/interchain-identifiers/v1",
  {
    "class": "did:ixo:entity:abc123"
  }
]
```

#### Resolving Contexts

The context properties are processed when resolving the IXO DID to retrieve the linked data properties for each specified JSON-LD context. By applying these contexts in sequential order, this defines all of the properties used in the IID Document. It allows for unambiguous extensibility, without the need for a centralized registry of DID methods and vocabulary.

### 4.3 Off-Chain Resources

IXO DIDs also provide a consistent, verifiable approach for referring to off-chain resources, whether these resources are digital, physical, or conceptual.

To achieve this, IXO DID implementations SHOULD use content-derived identifiers and MAY use content-derived addressing systems, in which the cryptographic hash of a linked resource is used to unambiguously verify the resource.

Standards such as Multihash [[MULTIHASH]](#ref-multihash) provide a wide range of hashing algorithm options and representations which can be used for this purpose.

Mechanisms such as Hashgraphs enable an arbitrary number of verifiable resources to be linked to a digital Domain, without revealing the number or content of these resources. Content Identifiers (CIDs) provide robust verifiability and enable resources to be addressed without path-dependency. Both mechanisms enable rich data to be associated with digital resources, without compromising privacy.

## 5. DID Document Examples

### Example 1: A Minimal DID Document (JSON-LD)

The following is a minimal DID Document from the DID Core specification:

```json
{
  "@context": "https://www.w3.org/ns/did/v1",
  "id": "did:example:abc123",
  "authentication": [{
    "id": "did:example:abc123#keys-1",
    "type": "Ed25519VerificationKey2020",
    "controller": "did:example:abc123",
    "publicKeyMultibase": "zH3C2AVvLMv6gmMNam3uVAjZpfkcJCwDwnZn6z3wXmqPV"
  }]
}
```

This minimal DID Document is fully conformant with the specification and includes a single verification relationship and method: how to authenticate on behalf of the DID Subject.

### Example 2: A Minimal IXO DID Document

```json
{
  "@context": [
    "https://www.w3.org/ns/did/v1",
    "https://w3id.org/ixo/ns/interchain-identifiers/v1"
  ],
  "id": "did:ixo:abc123",
  "authentication": [{
    "id": "did:ixo:abc123#keys-1",
    "type": "Ed25519VerificationKey2020",
    "controller": "did:ixo:abc123",
    "publicKeyMultibase": "zH3C2AVvLMv6gmMNam3uVAjZpfkcJCwDwnZn6z3wXmqPV"
  }]
}
```

Like Example 1, this minimal IXO DID Document provides for cryptographic authentication on behalf of the IID Asset. The second context entry pulls in the IXO JSON-LD context that indicates this DID Document is an IID Document. Literally, it means that the property definitions from the IID context — which are defined in this specification — apply to the properties of this DID Document, making it an IID Document.

IXO DID subjects are always on-chain assets, such as Non-Fungible Tokens. Authenticating on a token's behalf is taken to indicate proof-of-ownership. Since most on-chain Domains already employ some form of cryptographic proof-of-control for exercising ownership rights, an IXO DID Document of this kind should be readily producible for just about any asset on the IXO blockchain.

### Example 3: A Minimal, Privacy-Preserving IXO DID Document

```json
{
  "@context": [
    "https://www.w3.org/ns/did/v1",
    "https://w3id.org/ixo/ns/interchain-identifiers/v1"
  ],
  "id": "did:ixo:abc123",
  "authentication": [{
    "id": "did:ixo:abc123#keys-1",
    "type": "Ed25519VerificationKey2020",
    "controller": "did:ixo:abc123",
    "publicKeyMultibase": "zH3C2AVvLMv6gmMNam3uVAjZpfkcJCwDwnZn6z3wXmqPV"
  }],
  "service": [{
    "id": "did:ixo:abc123#mediator",
    "type": "polymorphicMediator2021",
    "serviceEndpoint": "http://8zd335ae47dp89pd.onion/iid/mediator/did:ixo:abc123"
  }],
  "linkedResource": [{
    "id": "did:ixo:abc123#resourceHashgraph",
    "path": "did:ixo:abc123/resourceHashgraph",
    "type": "hashgraph",
    "proof": "afybeiemxf5abjwjbikoz4mcb3a3dla6ual3jsgpdr4cjr3oz",
    "endpoint": "did:ixo:abc123?service=mediator"
  }]
}
```

There are four privacy-preserving elements in this minimal privacy-preserving IXO DID Document.

**NOTE:** From a privacy perspective, it might be preferable to make the following elements mandatory for IXO DIDs to maximize herd privacy. However, the current language of "SHOULD" for these requirements allows IID creators to trade off herd privacy when deemed appropriate for their use case. This tradeoff is discouraged, but allowed.

**First**, there is one and only one service endpoint, which points to a shared, polymorphic mediator, as described in Section 10.6 Service Privacy from the DID Core Specification. Service endpoints are optional. But to support herd privacy, it is a recommended practice to use one, and only one, service endpoint, which is a SHOULD requirement for IXO DIDs. For maximum privacy, this specification further recommends that the service endpoint specifies a Tor address for a polymorphic service capable of providing a number of functions such as a negotiator, mediator, or confidential storage.

> *It is the goal of herd privacy to ensure that the nature of specific subjects is obscured by the population of the whole. To maximize herd privacy, implementers need to rely on one — and only one — service endpoint, with that endpoint providing a proxy or mediator service that the controller is willing to depend on to protect such associations and that blinds requests to the ultimate service.*

**Second**, the service endpoint is a Tor endpoint, which provides enhanced network security, mitigating the ability for network observers to detect attempts to access that endpoint. The underlying service is just like any other web service. Using Tor obfuscates the networking layer without changing the service or API that runs over that secure connection. In some cases, this may also provide geographic shielding.

**Third**, there is one, and only one, linked resource entry which is capable of verifying an unknown number of associated resources. The entry type of `"hashgraph"` means that the proof is a Merkle tree of hashes of the underlying resources. Anyone who gathers the appropriate resources can independently verify their completeness and authenticity by comparing the hashgraph of the combined resources with the proof property in the linked resource definition. For instance, anyone considering buying an NFT could be cryptographically assured they have all the resources they need to confirm the NFT is what they believe it to be.

For herd privacy, it is preferred that all IXO DIDs use one, and only one, service endpoint and one, and only one, Linked Resource. In cases where there is no appropriate service endpoint, it is still preferable to point to a mediator. To support this, we encourage a multitude of publicly accessible mediator services offering a free "null response" service which is indistinguishable from a failed access from lack of authorization.

NOTE: It is acceptable to have multiple resources listed, but this may reduce privacy through adding more points of correlation.

**Fourth**, the single Linked Resource property specifies its endpoint as the IID's single service endpoint, using a properly formed DID-URL with a service parameter. This pattern allows every resource to use the same endpoint without duplicating endpoint URLs, even when specifying multiple linked resources.

## 6. DID Operations

This section defines the four DID operations for `did:ixo`: Create, Read, Update, and Deactivate. All write operations are performed through transactions on the IXO blockchain, a Cosmos SDK-based distributed ledger.

The IXO blockchain exposes the following interfaces:

- **Transaction API**: Standard Cosmos SDK transaction submission for Create, Update, and Deactivate operations.
- **gRPC Query API**: For resolving DID Documents via `ixo.iid.v1beta1.Query/IIDDocument`.
- **REST API**: For resolving DID Documents via `GET /ixo/iid/v1beta1/iidDocuments/{id}`.

### 6.1 Create

To create a `did:ixo` DID:

1. **Generate a key pair** using a supported cryptographic algorithm (Ed25519 or Secp256k1).
2. **Determine the method-specific identifier** for the Domain type being created.
3. **Construct the initial IXO DID Document** conforming to the structure defined in this specification, including the `@context`, `id`, `verificationMethod`, and any relevant verification relationships.
4. **Sign and submit a `MsgCreateIIDDocument` transaction** to the IXO blockchain, including the initial IXO DID Document and any associated entity metadata.
5. **Await confirmation** that the transaction has been successfully included in the blockchain.

Upon successful inclusion, the IXO blockchain associates the DID with the on-chain entity record. The DID is permanently assigned and can be resolved by any party with access to the IXO blockchain.

Off-chain pre-generation of the identifier (prior to on-chain registration) is RECOMMENDED, allowing the controller to establish the DID before committing it to the chain. The DID MUST be registered on-chain before it is resolvable.

### 6.2 Read (Resolve)

To resolve a `did:ixo` DID to its DID Document:

1. **Parse the DID** to extract the method-specific identifier.
2. **Query the IXO blockchain** using one of the following methods:
   - **gRPC**: Send a `QueryIIDDocumentRequest` to `ixo.iid.v1beta1.Query/IIDDocument` with the DID as the `id` parameter.
   - **REST**: Issue a `GET` request to `/ixo/iid/v1beta1/iidDocuments/{id}` on an IXO REST node endpoint.
3. **Apply JSON-LD contexts** specified in the `@context` property of the returned document.
4. **Return the resolved IXO DID Document** as the DID Document.

If the DID does not exist on the IXO blockchain, the resolver MUST return an error indicating the DID was not found.

If the DID Document has been deactivated, the resolver MUST return the DID Document with `deactivated: true` in the DID Document Metadata.

**DID Document Metadata** returned alongside the DID Document SHOULD include:

- `created`: The timestamp of the DID creation transaction.
- `updated`: The timestamp of the most recent update transaction.
- `deactivated`: Boolean indicating whether the DID has been deactivated.

### 6.3 Update

To update a `did:ixo` DID Document:

1. **Construct the updated IXO DID Document** with the desired property changes.
2. **Sign and submit a `MsgUpdateIIDDocument` transaction** to the IXO blockchain, including the updated IXO DID Document.
3. **Await confirmation** that the transaction has been successfully included in the blockchain.

Only the current controller(s) of the DID MAY submit update transactions. The controller MUST sign the transaction using a key specified in the current DID Document's `verificationMethod` or `capabilityInvocation` property.

### 6.4 Deactivate

To deactivate a `did:ixo` DID:

1. **Sign and submit a `MsgDeactivateIIDDocument` transaction** to the IXO blockchain.
2. **Await confirmation** that the transaction has been successfully included in the blockchain.

Upon successful deactivation, the IXO blockchain records the deactivation. Subsequent resolutions of the DID MUST return a DID Document with `deactivated: true` in the DID Document Metadata.

Deactivation is permanent on the IXO blockchain. Once deactivated, a `did:ixo` DID cannot be reactivated.

## 7. Use Cases

The IID specification was primarily designed to address the use-case requirements for Non-Fungible Tokens (NFTs), described in NFT-RFC-002 [[NFT-RFC-002]](#ref-nft-rfc-002). However, this specification is applicable to all types and classes of tokens, as well as other on-chain digital Domains.

## 8. Requirements

Implementations of this specification MUST achieve the following:

1. For interoperability, IXO DIDs MUST be conformant with the DID Core syntax for DIDs.

2. For discoverability, IXO DIDs MUST be able to refer to any Domain on the IXO blockchain.

3. For resolvability, IXO DID Documents MUST be conformant DID Documents in JSON-LD representation.

4. For composability, IXO DID Documents MUST be able to use custom properties in an unambiguous manner.

5. For dereferencing, IXO DID Documents MUST be able to define their own namespace for referring to off-chain Domains.

6. For privacy, IXO DID Documents SHOULD be able to specify how to verify, and optionally retrieve, Linked Resources without revealing the number or characteristics of the resources.

7. For self-sovereignty, IXO DIDs MUST be compatible with standards such as Verifiable Credentials, Authorization Capabilities, and Confidential Storage.

8. For applicability, IXO DIDs MUST be recognizable as an IXO DID through their syntax.

9. For usability, IXO DIDs MUST be compatible with existing DID tooling and infrastructure.

In addition to conforming with the requirements for DIDs:

10. All IXO DIDs MUST refer to a single on-chain Domain on the IXO blockchain, such as a token, smart contract, or account.

11. IXO DID Documents MUST be conformant DID Documents in JSON-LD representation. All properties used in an IXO DID Document MUST be conformant with DID JSON-LD processing rules, including the definition of such properties in JSON-LD context files specified by the `@context` property.

12. The IXO DID method SHOULD support off-chain creation of identifiers with on-chain registration.

## 9. Security Considerations

This section addresses security considerations for the `did:ixo` DID method, as required by the W3C DID Core specification [[DID-CORE]](#ref-did-core).

### 9.1 Cryptographic Key Security

Controllers of `did:ixo` DIDs MUST safeguard their private keys. Compromise of a private key allows an attacker to:

- Submit unauthorized update transactions to modify the DID Document.
- Authenticate as the DID Subject.
- Delegate or invoke capabilities on behalf of the DID Subject.

Implementers SHOULD use hardware security modules (HSMs) or secure enclaves for key storage in high-security applications. Key rotation SHOULD be performed regularly and whenever compromise is suspected.

### 9.2 Blockchain Network Security

The security of `did:ixo` DIDs depends on the security of the IXO blockchain. The IXO blockchain uses Byzantine Fault Tolerant (BFT) consensus, which provides security guarantees as long as fewer than one-third of validators (by stake weight) are malicious or faulty.

Implementers SHOULD:

- Use multiple independent IXO node endpoints for resolution to mitigate single-node compromise.
- Verify transaction inclusion proofs when resolving DID Documents.
- Monitor for significant changes in the validator set that could affect security guarantees.

### 9.3 JSON-LD Context Integrity

IXO DID Documents reference JSON-LD contexts that define the meaning of properties. Implementers SHOULD:

- Cache JSON-LD contexts to mitigate availability attacks on context servers.
- Verify that context URLs match expected values and have not been tampered with.
- Resolve context URLs over HTTPS with certificate validation, or from a content-addressed system (e.g., using a CID).

Be aware that changes to context definitions at the referenced URLs could affect the interpretation of IID Documents. For this reason, context URLs SHOULD reference immutable or versioned resources.

### 9.4 Service Endpoint and Resource Integrity

Service endpoints and Linked Resource endpoints specified in IXO DID Documents may be controlled by third parties. Implementers MUST:

- Treat responses from service endpoints as potentially untrusted.
- Verify resource integrity using the `proof` property in Linked Resource descriptors before using resources.
- Be aware that a compromised service endpoint could serve modified data.

### 9.5 Residual Risks

As with all DID methods, the following residual risks apply to `did:ixo`:

- **Sybil attacks**: Creation of many DIDs to pollute the namespace or overwhelm resolvers. Mitigated by on-chain transaction fees.
- **Correlation**: Multiple DIDs controlled by the same entity may be linkable through on-chain transaction analysis, even when using privacy-preserving features.
- **Phishing**: Attackers may create DIDs whose method-specific identifiers visually resemble legitimate DIDs. Applications MUST present the full DID to users for verification.
- **Key loss**: If a controller loses access to all keys listed in the DID Document, the DID may become permanently uncontrollable and unrecoverable.

## 10. Privacy Considerations

This section addresses privacy considerations for the `did:ixo` DID method, as required by the W3C DID Core specification [[DID-CORE]](#ref-did-core).

### 10.1 On-Chain Data Permanence

Data published to the IXO blockchain is permanent and publicly accessible. IXO DID Documents, once published, cannot be removed. Controllers MUST carefully consider what data is included in the IXO DID Document:

- All data in the DID Document becomes permanently and publicly accessible on-chain.
- Update transactions do not erase historical states; all previous states remain accessible through the blockchain history.

Controllers SHOULD minimize the data published on-chain and use Linked Resources with appropriate access controls for sensitive or personal data.

### 10.2 Linked Resources and Privacy

The `linkedResource` property supports privacy-preserving resource specification through hashgraph proofs. Controllers SHOULD:

- Use hashgraph proofs rather than exposing all resources directly, to obscure the quantity and type of linked resources from observers.
- Route resource retrieval through polymorphic mediators to prevent direct correlation between specific resources and their requesters.
- Use encryption for sensitive linked resources.

### 10.3 Herd Privacy

To maximize herd privacy:

- IXO DID Documents SHOULD specify exactly one service endpoint, which SHOULD be a polymorphic mediator.
- IXO DID Documents SHOULD specify exactly one Linked Resource, which SHOULD be a hashgraph resource descriptor.

This makes individual DID Documents difficult to distinguish from one another, protecting the privacy of DID subjects within the larger population of IXO DIDs.

### 10.4 Correlation Risks

Even with privacy-preserving features, the following correlation risks exist:

- IXO DID Documents are public, allowing observers to correlate DID activity with public blockchain events.
- Controller addresses on the IXO blockchain may be linkable to IXO DID Documents through on-chain transaction analysis.
- Multiple IXO DIDs controlled by the same address may be correlated through on-chain analysis.

Implementers SHOULD use separate keys for different DIDs and rotate keys regularly to reduce correlation risk.

### 10.5 Personal Data

Controllers SHOULD NOT include personal data directly in IXO DID Documents. If personal data must be associated with a DID:

- Use Verifiable Credentials stored as encrypted Linked Resources.
- Apply appropriate access controls through service endpoints.
- Obtain explicit consent from data subjects before associating personal data with a DID.

This specification does not define mechanisms for data erasure, as on-chain data is permanent. Controllers must consider this before publishing any potentially personal or sensitive information.
